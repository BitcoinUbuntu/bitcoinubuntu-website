The "Physics" That Forgot the Facts

A recent article tries to make the case that one particular mining stack comes out ahead over a 50–100-year horizon. While the ideas behind it – efficiency, latency and fees – are worth understanding on their own, we don't believe that the numbers and assumptions built on top of them hold up at all. What follows is our attempt to work through both.

@bitcoinubuntu 10 July 2026

The article gets a lot of the vocabulary right, and at first glance that can make it seem fairly persuasive. Take a closer look though, and it starts to fall apart. It opens with things that are genuinely fixed – SHA-256, the 21 million cap, the halving schedule – and uses these to frame a set of tables that project a mining setup's earnings all the way out to the year 2126, ranked by a "100-Year Score".

To get there it either overlooks or mishandles the things that actually shape those hundred years: it invokes the halving but its projections completely ignore it, it leaves out the transaction fees that eventually become the entire reward, it says nothing about network hashrate climbing or software upgrading, and it assumes hardware and energy costs stay fixed for a century. Any one of those is more than enough to sink a century-long projection, so the premise is built on assumptions that simply aren't true. Even so, we won't go through every one of them here; instead we'll pretend that we're living in the same alternative reality which the author presents in his thesis, and show that even granting him every convenient oversight, the numbers still don't hold up to the barest scrutiny.

The numbers and various assumptions that carry the argument are wrong, but they're wrong in ways that turn out to be useful to study, because each mistake points to something true about how mining actually works. So instead of just listing errors, we want to explain the mechanics as we go: the halving, a miner's share of the network, pool and transaction fees, stale shares. Once those are well understood, the mistakes are easy to spot. Where the article does get something right, we'll say so, but it's a short list, and mostly surface detail. Where it tries to draw conclusions is where it goes horribly wrong.

A quick note on method first. Every figure below comes from a single model, which we've set out in full at the end, using inputs anyone can check for themselves: the device's rated hashrate, the current network hashrate, and Bitcoin's issuance schedule. Where we had to assume something, we've used whatever is most generous to the author's own conclusions, so if anything the corrections here are on the conservative side.

01 The concept of convenient omission

The halving matters, as do fees

Miners get paid in two ways: a block subsidy, which is newly issued coins, and the transaction fees in the block. Today the subsidy is by far the larger of the two, but it doesn't stay constant forever. Every 210,000 blocks, which works out to roughly four years, the subsidy is cut in half. It started out at 50 BTC per block back in 2009 and sits at 3.125 BTC today. From there it drops to 1.5625 at the next halving, then 0.78125, and so on, until the last tiny fraction of a coin is issued somewhere around 2140.

The important thing to note about the issuance schedule is how front-loaded it is. Each epoch issues half as much as the one before, so the coins come early and then thin out quickly. By around 2040, somewhere near 99% of all the bitcoin there will ever be will have already been mined. The whole century from 2040 to 2140 only issues the last little sliver.

There's an easy way to see just how little. Because each epoch pays half of the one before it, everything still left to mine after any halving adds up to no more than that single epoch which has just passed. So from the very next halving, barely two years away, everything mined across almost the entire hundred years the author projects will add up to less than this current epoch pays on its own.

Spot the flaw

In the article's tables, every 100-year figure is exactly double the 50-year one: 515,000 becomes 1,030,000 sats, 4,288 becomes 8,576 BTC. The only way doubling the time doubles the income is if the block reward never changes. So the author has simply neglected to factor the halving in at all. The projection assumes the second fifty years pay just as much as the first, when in reality they will pay almost nothing.

02 The concept of network share

A miner can't reliably earn more than its share of the network

What a miner earns isn't really a property of the machine on its own. On average it's a share: the miner's hashrate divided by the total network hashrate, times whatever the network is paying out. Any single block comes down to luck, but over years, let alone a century, the real total settles to almost exactly that share. So a miner who controls a millionth of the world's hashpower earns roughly a millionth of the coins. A pool, a gateway or a template policy can slightly nudge the edges, and which transactions a miner chooses to include can certainly move transaction fee income too, but none of those change the underlying share of hashpower.

Right now the network runs at roughly 873 EH/s, and a Bitaxe Gamma 601 adds 1.2 TH/s to that. Divide one by the other and the device is about 0.0000001374% of the network, which at today's block subsidy works out to somewhere in the region of 60 satoshis a day. That's not guesswork, it's calculated directly from those public statistics, and independent profitability estimators (miningnow, asicminervalue) land in the same range.

That points to something else the article misses: the share is a ceiling, not just a rate. There are only about 950,000 BTC of subsidy left to be mined, and over its whole life a miner can collect only its share of that fixed pot. Luck moves any single block, but across a lifetime it barely moves the total. Efficiencies can help claw back the tiny fraction lost due to stale work, but nothing outside of extraordinary luck lifts a miner above that share.

That ceiling is where the article's bigger tables really come undone, and more badly than the small one. Take the largest (Table 2c), for a roughly 60 EH/s operation:

However you frame it, the table pays this miner more bitcoin than its share of the network could ever produce.

The mid-size table, "Table 2b" for a 5 EH/s farm, has the same problem, and by the same ratio: it claims 8,576 BTC where the halving-aware ceiling is about 5,450, again roughly 1.6 times too high. There's also an odd inconsistency across the three tables. The little Bitaxe is credited with about five times more per unit of hashrate than the big farms are. Corrected, the figure is the same at every scale, about 109,000 sats of lifetime subsidy per terahash, because earnings scale linearly with hashrate. For the article's own tables to disagree with each other by five times, they can't have come from a single consistent model.

The same tables carry further inconsistencies. Each one lists a stale rate, then a "% Loss" that is meant to follow from it, and the two don't line up. The loss is measured against the top row, which runs at roughly 0% stale, so the 0.02% pool should give up about 0.02% and the 0.25% legacy pool about 0.25%. Instead every table shows 0.18% for the 0.02% row, nine times what the stale rate allows, and around half a percent for the 0.25% row, up to roughly double. The stale rate is the only thing that changes down each table, so the loss can't be larger than the rate that causes it. Add to this that the same 0.25% legacy pool is marked 0.40% in the Bitaxe table, 0.54% in the farm table and 0.49% in the largest – three different answers to a single input, when a percentage loss from a fixed stale rate doesn't depend on the size of the miner.

03 The concept of made-up numbers

The "100-Year Score" that looks academic but isn't

The article opens with a table that ranks six mining setups by a "100-Year Score". The stack it promotes lands at 9.8 out of 10, the next is rated at 9.4, on down to legacy Stratum at 4.1. Such precise values look like the output of a model, and we suspect that's the intent. But there is no transparent model disclosed. No methodology is given, no weights, no inputs, nothing that would let anyone else reproduce the numbers for themselves or understand how they were derived.

The article's ranking table, as published.

Precision with nothing to back it lends the look of legitimacy to what appears to be little more than a personal preference. The placements are telling. The two top scores, 9.8 and 9.4, belong to the exact setups the article goes on to recommend, and the lowest belong to the competition, described with labels like "higher stale" and "spam tax" rather than any measurable statistics.

04 The concept of stale shares

The stale share penalty is minuscule and temporary

A stale share is one which a miner finds a moment too late, after the network has already moved on to the next block. It earns nothing, so lower latency and locally built templates, which cut stale work, are a genuine benefit of the tooling the article promotes. We'll grant it that. The real question is how big the effect is. The article puts legacy setups at 0.25% stale and local-template setups at roughly zero, but that 0.25% doesn't come from any independent measurement which we can find. It's the exact example Ocean's own DATUM marketing uses ("from even 0.25% before to nearly 0% with DATUM"), and it's close to the Stratum V2 project's own figure of about 99.8% share acceptance improving towards 100% with local job declaration. Both are best-case vendor numbers rather than evidence about how a given pool actually performs.

Even at face value, it's the opposite of decisive. A 0.25% stale rate means keeping 99.75% of revenue, and as we saw earlier, the article's own loss column somehow overstates that. Over the corrected lifetime of our little Bitaxe, the gap between the "optimal" stack and the "avoid" legacy pool comes to about 330 satoshis in subsidy rewards, spread across a hundred years. That's roughly what a miner earns from the fee on a single low-fee-paying transaction included in a block.

The bigger problem here is the strange assumption underlying the whole comparison: that a "legacy" pool stays legacy for a hundred years. It won't. The low-latency, local-template tooling the article is promoting is open source and simple to adopt, and any pool actually losing a quarter of a percent to stale work would close that gap quickly, because nothing is stopping it. It's an invented disadvantage stacked on top of the halving error: first it pretends the reward never falls, then it pretends the competition never improves.

Two more problems with the "compounding" story

First, a flat percentage loss doesn't compound as the author would have us believe. It's the same 0.25% every year, not an ever-shrinking pile.

Second, if sub-1% edges were really that decisive, the article would have to admit that the one genuinely large lever it mentions, Ocean's very own pool fee, dwarfs them all. Which brings us to the fee.

05 The concept of permanent discounts

The pool fee isn't physics and has already changed

The article leans on a real perk. Miners who run DATUM, an open-source tool for building block templates locally, get a 50% discount on Ocean's pool fee, paying 1% instead of the standard 2%. That discount is genuine. But the projection treats it as a fixed constant for a century, and it isn't.

Start with where the fee actually originates. DATUM, the protocol, is fee-agnostic. All it does is build templates locally and coordinate the reward split with the pool, and in its own documentation it "is not specific to a pooled reward system". The fee and the discount aren't defined by DATUM at all. They're set by the pool operator as a matter of commercial policy, and Ocean's own materials frame DATUM as the technology and the operator as the one controlling pricing. A different pool could run the identical protocol and charge something completely different.

Ocean has already exercised that control. The pool launched in November 2023 with 0% fees, then in mid-November 2024 introduced the current structure of 2%, or 1% with DATUM. That was its first fee change, roughly one year after launch. So a core input to a 100-year projection is a private company's revocable pricing decision, one that has already moved once in the pool's roughly two-and-a-half years of existence.

Nothing in Bitcoin's "immutable math" stops Ocean from raising the base fee, trimming the discount or restructuring its payout system tomorrow, and a century guarantees many such decisions will be made if the company survives that long. The article treats Ocean's current fee as if it were as permanent as SHA-256. One is written into the protocol, the other is a company's pricing that already changed once and can be changed again.

06 The concept of a self-defeating cure

Filtering forfeits fees that are mining's future revenue

The article calls arbitrary data a "silent tax on hashes per joule". That gets the physics backwards. A joule is spent computing a hash, and it costs the same whatever transactions fill the block, so template policy changes what a miner includes, not how efficiently it hashes. More to the point, Bitcoin already has a mechanism for junk, and it's the very one which the article claims to revere. Block space is scarce and fixed. As adoption grows and that space is contested, we can expect that people paying real money to move real value will outbid low-value data for it. The fee market is the filter, it answers to no committee.

There's a bigger problem which the article never addresses. A transaction fee is a miner's income, not its cost, so a miner who screens valid transactions out of its blocks simply earns less than one who leaves them in, and the fees it declines are picked up by the next operator who isn't so fussy. When block space is contested, which is the very high-value future the article projects, filtering valid transactions is a standing decision to earn less than the competition. The "spam rejection" which the article lists among its top stack's decisive advantages is, in fee terms, a self-imposed handicap.

Let's also be clear about what BIP-110 actually is, because the article raises it alongside Stratum V2 as though the two belonged to one modernisation effort. They don't. Stratum V2 is a transport protocol, it changes how miners talk to pools. BIP-110 is a hotly contested soft fork to Bitcoin's consensus rules. One's plumbing, the other's politics.

None of which is meant to defend spam. The trouble is that the cure which the article's author champions, Bitcoin Knots-style filters and the BIP-110 soft fork, is ineffective and corrosive:

Ineffective, because a determined data-stuffer just routes around the size limits. Peter Todd embedded the entire BIP-110 text into a BIP-110-compliant transaction, and a separate demonstration stored a 60 KB image using none of the filtered paths. The inscription ecosystem has already built its escape hatch, more to demonstrate the filter's futility than in any expectation that the fork actually activates. The reference Ordinals implementation has an approved BIP-110-compatible inscription envelope queued to merge the instant the rule activates, a companion parser already reads it, and a public readiness list tracks the wider ecosystem. The fork would activate already circumvented.

It's also corrosive because of what it would do if it worked. A protocol that can retroactively rule valid transactions unacceptable and soft-fork them away is a protocol that no serious builder can rely on. Why commit to a vault, an inheritance scheme or a Layer-2 rail if tomorrow's rules might brand the outputs they depend on as "spam"? That uncertainty is the real damage. It discourages engineers from building the long-horizon monetary use cases the article says it wants. The lack of support is clear: at the time of writing, signalling sits under 1%, far below even the 55% that would trigger early activation. But low support won't stop it. BIP-110 is written to lock in regardless, with a mandatory flag-day that rejects any block not enforcing the new rules, and forcing a contested change onto an unwilling network this way is exactly what could result in a chain split.

So the article projects a hundred-year, fee-funded future while backing a tool that neither stops "spam" nor lets anyone reliably build the monetary economy which that future depends on. Wanting less junk on-chain is reasonable. Forking the protocol into permanent uncertainty to combat it ineffectively is not "immutable physics", it's a reckless political bet.

07 The concept of borrowed authority

"Triple verification" proves the work, not the payment

The article describes a "triple verification of hashrate" that supposedly gives "cryptographic certainty" a miner is paid for all its work. The idea is to match three logs: what a hashrate-rental provider says it sent, what the miner's own gateway recorded, and what the pool credited. For anyone renting hashrate, reconciling those three is sensible practice. Each share in the gateway's own log is a checkable piece of proof-of-work, so a renter can confirm the provider actually delivered the hashpower it sold.

But that's where the cryptography ends. The shares prove the work was delivered; nothing proves the payment. Matching three tallies is reconciliation; nothing in it cryptographically binds the pool to pay. The payout still depends on the pool's reward accounting, on block luck, and on the fee policy we covered a moment ago, none of which the three agreeing logs can prove honest. The DATUM software's own documentation even notes that share counts on mining hardware "may not perfectly match" the pool's. Calling that "cryptographic certainty" misrepresents it.

08 In fairness

What the article does get right

A good-faith reading has to credit the parts that hold up, and there are some. The entities are real and mostly described accurately: Ocean is a real pool, DATUM is real open-source software, Bitcoin Knots is a real node implementation, and the 50% DATUM fee discount is genuine (with the caveats above about who controls it). The history is right, too: Stratum V1 dates to around 2012 (from Marek "Slush" Palatinus), and Stratum V2 was indeed pioneered by the Braiins team in 2019, with Matt Corallo. The article's report that seven major pools joined the Stratum V2 Working Group on 7 May 2026 checks out. It's worth noting who they are though: they include Foundry and AntPool, the pair the article's own ranking parks second from bottom at 5.9 out of 10. The pool operators it rates worst are among those driving the very standard it rates best. The AtlasPool detail checks out too: the pool announced a migration to Knots across five of its nine endpoints in late May 2026.

The one table we'd leave almost untouched is the Stratum V1-vs-V2 comparison. Its headline figures (roughly 60–70% less bandwidth, encryption, job negotiation, "up to 7.4%" efficiency) come straight from the Stratum V2 project's own published materials and are reported accurately. The same goes for the "95% bandwidth reduction" credited to DATUM, which traces to Ocean's own pitch for remote sites, a drop from roughly 200 GB to 8 GB per day for a 1 EH/s operation. These are vendor benchmarks, and "up to 7.4%" is a best-case scenario rather than a typical one, but at least these figures aren't fabricated out of wishful thinking.

Local template construction, whether via DATUM or Stratum V2's job declaration, is a real improvement for decentralisation and it does reduce stale work. The intuition that efficiency and latency matter to margins is sound. On fees, the article misses the other half of the ledger: the "spam" it wants filtered pays transaction fees, and those fees are a miner's income, especially once the block subsidy is mostly gone. Filtering them out cuts margins rather than protecting them. It's the one "edge" we can't credit even in part, because it patently isn't one.

The problem is that these true things are used to prop up projections that fall apart under scrutiny.

The bottom line

Strip away the tables and the article's real claims are modest and only partly true: run efficient, low-latency, sovereign infrastructure and a miner will shave small percentages off its losses. Absolutely worth doing. But the article doesn't sell "small percentages". The trouble is that it sells a 50–100-year destiny told in precise satoshis, a story which requires switching off the halving, ignoring the ceiling a miner's own hashrate imposes, treating a company's pricing as a constant of nature, and filtering away fees that are mining's future. The physics at the top of the article is real. The article invokes the laws, but goes on to ignore most of the facts.

Method & sources

The model. One calculation underlies every corrected figure. A miner's constant network share f = its hashrate ÷ total network hashrate (873 EH/s, the current level, held constant, which is the assumption most generous to the article, since network hashrate has historically only grown). Lifetime subsidy = f summed against the real block-reward schedule (3.125 BTC now, halving every 210,000 blocks) from mid-2026 to the end of issuance (~2140). Total remaining subsidy issuance from now ≈ 951,000 BTC (rounded to ~950,000 in the text). Corrected lifetime subsidy works out to ~109,000 sats per terahash at every scale: ~130,800 sats for the 1.2 TH/s Bitaxe, ~5,450 BTC for 5 EH/s, ~65,400 BTC for 60 EH/s. Transaction fees are excluded throughout (they're unknowable over a century, and, as discussed, they're the transactions the article proposes to filter). The exact daily-earnings figure depends on the network-hashrate assumption, but the conclusion doesn't turn on it. Even if the Bitaxe earned double our estimate, say the network were only half today's size, the claim would still be about 4× too high, and the years 50→100 would still add almost nothing.
